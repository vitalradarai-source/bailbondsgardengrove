ICE Hold + Bail Bonds in Garden Grove — What Families Need to Know

TL;DR

  • An ICE detainer (Form I-247A) asks the jail to hold someone up to 48 hours for federal immigration custody
  • California's SB 54 limits when local jails honor ICE detainers — most misdemeanors are not held
  • You can still post bail on the criminal charge, but ICE may take custody if a detainer is active
  • The 10% bail premium is non-refundable even if ICE takes the defendant
  • Always consult both a criminal defense attorney AND an immigration attorney before posting
  • Garden Grove arrests transfer to OC Sheriff custody at OC Intake/Release Center, 550 N The City Dr, Orange

An immigration hold complicates an already stressful situation. Families in Garden Grove who learn that a loved one has been arrested AND has an ICE detainer face decisions that involve both criminal and federal immigration law — two completely separate legal systems. This guide explains what an immigration hold is, what it means for bail, and the critical steps to take before posting any bond.

This article is general information, not legal advice. ICE hold cases require both a criminal defense attorney and an immigration attorney. Do not make decisions based solely on this guide.

What an ICE Detainer Actually Is

An ICE detainer — officially called Form I-247A — is a request from U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement to a local jail. It asks the jail to hold a person for up to 48 hours past their criminal release time so ICE can take them into federal custody for immigration removal proceedings.

Important: a detainer is a request, not a court-ordered warrant. Local jails decide whether to honor it. The default federal policy is that detainers can be honored, but state and local laws can limit how often they actually are.

California's SB 54 (California Values Act)

California's Senate Bill 54, signed in 2017, restricts local cooperation with ICE. It prohibits state and local law enforcement from honoring ICE detainers except in specific circumstances:

  • Defendant has a serious or violent felony conviction within the past 15 years
  • Current charge is a serious or violent felony as defined in CA PC §1192.7 or §667.5
  • Specific aggravated felonies (sex crimes against minors, serious gang-related crimes)
  • Federal warrant signed by a judge (different from a detainer)

For most misdemeanors and many lower-level felonies, OC Sheriff's Department will not hold the defendant past their criminal release time. The defendant may walk out and ICE may not take custody at the jail.

However: ICE can arrest someone separately at the courthouse, in the community, at home, or at work — even when SB 54 prevents jail-to-ICE transfer. Posting bail does not protect against community ICE enforcement.

What Happens When You Post Bail With an ICE Hold

Three possible outcomes:

  1. SB 54 protects release: The detainer is for an offense not covered by SB 54. The defendant is released to family per the criminal release process.
  2. SB 54 allows transfer: The detainer covers a serious felony or qualifying past conviction. The defendant is transferred to ICE custody — typically to the Adelanto ICE Processing Center or other federal facility.
  3. ICE arrest in the community: The defendant is released from the jail but ICE arrests them at the courthouse exit, on the way home, or later.

The bail bond premium (10% under California Insurance Code §1800.4) is non-refundable in all three scenarios — the bondsman has earned the fee by posting the surety bond. If the defendant ends up in ICE custody and misses a state court date, the bondsman may file for bond exoneration, but this requires legal process.

Critical Steps Before Posting Bail With an ICE Hold

  1. Confirm the ICE detainer. Call the booking facility (OC Intake/Release Center, (714) 935-7905) and ask whether an ICE detainer has been filed. Get the detainer number if possible.
  2. Hire a criminal defense attorney. They handle the underlying state charge.
  3. Hire an immigration attorney. They handle the federal removal proceedings — this is a separate specialty. Free legal aid is available through organizations like the Public Counsel Immigrant Rights Project ((213) 385-2977) and the Immigrant Defenders Law Center ((213) 634-0999).
  4. Discuss strategy. Sometimes posting bail isn't the right move. If ICE will likely take custody anyway, fighting the criminal case from custody (where ICE can't easily transfer mid-case) may be better than posting and ending up in Adelanto.
  5. Understand the financial exposure. The 10% premium is non-refundable. Collateral pledged as part of the bond may be at risk if the defendant misses court due to ICE detention.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is an ICE detainer?

Form I-247A — a federal request asking the local jail to hold someone up to 48 hours past their criminal release time for ICE custody. A request, not a court warrant.

Can I bail someone out with an ICE hold?

Yes — bail can be posted on the criminal charge. But ICE may still take custody if SB 54 doesn't protect the defendant. Always consult both a criminal defense and immigration attorney first.

What happens if ICE takes them after I post bail?

The 10% bail premium is non-refundable per CA Ins Code §1800.4. The defendant transfers to a federal facility. Missed state court dates may require bond exoneration filings.

Does Garden Grove cooperate with ICE?

Garden Grove transfers to OC Sheriff custody. Under SB 54, OC SD honors detainers only for serious or violent felonies — most misdemeanors are not held.

What should families do first?

Hire BOTH a criminal defense attorney AND an immigration attorney. Confirm the detainer with the booking facility. Discuss whether posting bail is strategically the right move.
